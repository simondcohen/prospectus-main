Report to the creditors of the bankruptcy of the company Alfred Ikelmer & Co., whose business is in the music trade and whose registered office is in Paris, [unclear] Street No. 11; By Mr. Sautton, final trustee of the bankruptcy (Article 506 of the Commercial Code). Gentlemen creditors, You are summoned by the Judge-Commissioner to deliberate on the establishment of a concordat between yourselves and the company Alfred Ikelmer & Co., composed of Messrs. Alfred Isaac Ikelmer and Joseph Désiré Ikelmer, and on your formation as a creditors’ committee. In accordance with Article 506 of the Commercial Code, I have the honor to submit to you my report on the current state of the bankruptcy, on the formalities that have been completed, and on the operations that have taken place. The bankruptcy of the company Alfred Ikelmer & Co. was declared by judgment of the Commercial Court of the Seine, dated September 19, 1860, at the request of Mr. Gauthier, one of the creditors, under the circumstances which I will indicate below. This judgment included in the same bankruptcy, as part of the same company, the two brothers Alfred and Désiré Ikelmer, the elder, who had carried on a commission business in goods in Paris, at 29 Provence Street; but upon the opposition raised, on the one hand by a creditor of the company Alfred Ikelmer & Co., and on the other by a creditor of Mr. Ikelmer the elder, a corrective judgment was rendered on October 27, 1861, which declared that the company Alfred Ikelmer & Co. consisted solely of the two brothers Alfred and Désiré Ikelmer. The same judgment, while affirming the declaration of bankruptcy of Mr. Ikelmer the elder, ordered that the proceedings in that bankruptcy be conducted separately. This company is a de facto partnership that has not been formalized by any deed and has not been published. It dates from October 1856. Continuation of the significant losses suffered on operations in England, the two brothers ceased their commission and merchandise business, and transferred one-third of the shop to a third brother, Mr. Adolphe Ikelmer, to devote themselves exclusively to the music trade. From January 1858 to May following, they acquired the following: From Messrs. Benacia, former music publisher, various musical assets, for 18,000 francs; From Messrs. Gaden, a music‐trade business, for 12,000 francs; And finally from Mr. Ledentu, former music publisher, a business for 65,000 francs; Total: 95,000 francs. These acquisitions included at once the music engraving plates and the items attached to those musical assets. The price was payable by promissory notes in the installments specified below, in accordance with the practice of the time. The Ikelmer brothers lacked the resources to undertake these transactions; moreover, they had incurred a certain liability by assuming some of their father’s debts; on the other hand, the loss incurred on their export business in England had created an even larger liability, the exact amount of which would now be difficult to determine; the acquisition of the businesses and musical assets I have just described further overburdened their situation. They were solvent only until August 1860, and then only by means you already know and which I must recall here. Mr. Alfred Ikelmer, having been appointed manager of the company, had been employed as a bookkeeper at the firm Xavier Jouvin & Cie; despite this post, he continued to direct that firm. Following the financial crisis of 1857, Xavier Jouvin & Cie entrusted him with arranging the negotiation of its paper with Parisian bankers, and he managed these negotiations from November 1857 until April 1858. In his own business, Mr. Alfred Ikelmer entrusted to some accountants initiatives. He created forged securities on which he affixed the false signature of the firm Xavier Jouvin & Co., and negotiated them with various bankers who had previously accepted the genuine signature of that firm and accepted these papers without suspicion. These forged securities consisted of drafts drawn by the firm Xavier Jouvin & Co. and accepted by persons who could be considered clients with business relations with it; they were payable, one in London, the other in Bordeaux, or even in Marseille, and the funds in cash were paid into the account by sending cash or securities to correspondents in charge of making the payment. But he could only procure the necessary funds by the same method, that is, by continuing to issue forged securities created under the same conditions; this circulation became exceedingly costly due to negotiation fees; he also had to add the cost of sending funds to the places of payment; so that Mr. Alfred Ikelmer, who at first had cherished the hope of gradually paying off this circulation with the profits of the music business, found himself, on the contrary, dragged into being able to meet his various commitments only as they matured, to a considerable extent. But a circulation of this nature necessarily had to lead to fatal consequences; towards the end of July 1860, one of the bankers to whom these securities had been discounted, Messrs.
