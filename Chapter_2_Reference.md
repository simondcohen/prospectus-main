## Primary Sources

### Commerson Lawsuit

- **Tribunal de commerce de la Seine**, April 1858

  - "Les Binettes contemporaines" quadrille case
  - *Concurrence déloyale* ruling - title/image theft vs. public musical property

- **Commerson's "Musique Frelatée" column** in *Le Tintamarre*

- **"Musique Frelatée: Un quadrille de Strauss condamné par le Tribunal de Commerce."** *Le Tintamarre*, April 18, 1858.

  - Full account of the Binettes contemporaines lawsuit (audience April 15)
  - Ikelmer reissued Strauss's "Cinq cents diables" quadrille (1855) with new title and Nadar portraits
  - Includes correspondence: Strauss disclaims involvement (Feb 28), Nadar asserts artistic property rights (Feb 23)
  - Tribunal judgment: 200 francs damages to Commerson for prejudice caused by title usurpation
  - Key phrase from Nadar: "je n'y ai plus aucun droit, et c'est à toi seul de protéger la chose qui est tienne et uniquement tienne" (on the drawings)
  - Nadar's artistic property axiom: "La propriété artistique est une propriété"
  - Reveals tension between musical works as public property vs. visual art/titles as protected property

- **Coverage of Commerson case.** *La Vérité pour tous*, May 13, 1858.

  - Reprints/discusses Commerson's "Musique Frelatée" column from *Le Tintamarre*
  - Shows wider circulation of the case beyond satirical press
  - Demonstrates public interest in artistic property disputes
  - Published nearly a month after the original, following the tribunal judgment

- **"Juridiction Commerciale: Tribunal de Commerce de la Seine - Les Binettes Contemporaines."** *Le Droit: Journal des Tribunaux*, April 19-20, 1858.

  - Official tribunal judgment report (audience April 15, 1858)
  - Key legal principle: "Un éditeur de quadrilles n'a pas le droit de s'emparer d'un titre et des portraits d'une publication existante"
  - Details: Ikelmer republished Strauss's "Cinq cents diables" with Commerson's title "Binettes contemporaines"
  - Court found this caused prejudice to Commerson, violating his collection rights for "Trente binettes pour 1 franc"
  - Judgment: Ikelmer must cease using the title and vignettes; pay 200 francs damages plus costs
  - Establishes that while quadrilles may be public domain, titles and visual elements retain protection

- **"Art. 323. Titre et désignation - Portraits - Publications différentes - Binettes contemporaines."** *Annales de la propriété industrielle, artistique et littéraire*, 1858: 223-224. <https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k6158638t/f227.item.r=commerson#>

  - Legal digest entry codifying the Commerson v. Ikelmer case as precedent
  - Principle: "Un éditeur de musique n'a pas le droit d'appliquer à des quadrilles ou autres compositions musicales le titre adopté par un auteur pour une publication littéraire"
  - Case summary: Commerson published biographical series "Binettes contemporaines" (1854) with Nadar portraits
  - Ikelmer reused title for Strauss quadrille originally called "Cinq cents diables"
  - Added portraits of Dumas père et fils and Théophile Gautier (also by Nadar)
  - Initial demand: 1,500 francs damages; tribunal awarded 200 francs
  - Shows how case entered jurisprudential canon on artistic property rights

### Théâtre Lyrique Economics and Management

- **"A Travers les Théâtres."** *Le Figaro*, April 15, 1860.

  - Reports on Carvalho's departure from Théâtre-Lyrique
  - Charles Réty takes over direction
  - Details Carvalho's spending practices: "Quand M. Carvalho préparait un nouvel opéra, il faisait appel à tous les artistes peintres, dessinateurs et décorateurs, convoquait les armuriers, marchands de soieries et de velours, les passementiers, etc.; achetait, commandait avec enthousiasme"
  - *Faust* production costs: "Faust fut magnifique de mise en scène; mais M. Carvalho avait dépensé 150 mille francs, c'est-à-dire cent vingt mille francs de trop"
  - Describes Carvalho's financial difficulties: passif of 250,000 francs, matériel valued at 400,000 francs

- **"Chronique Parisienne."** *Le Figaro*, April 8, 1860.

  - General commentary on theater economics and Parisian cultural life
  - Observations on entertainment industry costs and business practices

- **Pougin, Arthur.** "La millième de 'Faust'." *Le Ménestrel : journal de musique*, No. 50, 60me Année, December 16, 1894: 393-395.

  - Historical retrospective on Faust's creation and performance history
  - Details on initial reception and economic challenges at Théâtre-Lyrique (1859)
  - Notes on transformation from commercial failure to spectacular success
  - Documents the journey from 57 representations in first 3.5 years to 1000th performance milestone
  - Provides context on Gounod's initial difficulties getting the work staged
  - Chronicles shift from dialogue version at Théâtre-Lyrique to grand opera format

### Music Publishing Business Formation

- **"Société de l'Univers musical."** *Gazette des Tribunaux*, April 3, 1857.

  - Legal notice announcing formation of music publishing société en commandite
  - Émile-Guillaume Ledentu as sole responsible partner (gérant)
  - Capital: 40,000 francs (400 shares at 100F each)
  - Object: publication of *L'Univers musical* journal (founded 1853) and music sales
  - Ledentu receives 150 shares for contributing journal property and subscriber list
  - Located at boulevard des Italiens, 6
  - Shows typical structure of music publishing ventures in this period

- **"Société de l'Univers musical - Constitution définitive."** *Gazette des Tribunaux*, May 3, 1857.

  - Follow-up notice confirming definitive constitution as of April 16, 1857
  - Confirms subscription of all shares with 25% paid up front
  - General assembly (April 15) approved Ledentu's 150-share attribution and 20% profit share
  - Lists of shareholders and surveillance council members attached to notarial act
  - Shows formal process of société formation: announcement → subscription → constitution

## Music Publishing Context

### Brandus & Cie (est. 1846)

- **Origins & reorganisation** – Began when Louis Brandus bought Maurice Schlesinger's Paris firm, including *Revue et Gazette musicale*. Initially a société en commandite; debts forced Brandus to bring in partners. In 1854 investor Selim Dufour injected capital and gained control; by **1858 the company had several shareholders and a formal board.**
- **Outcome** – Heavy legacy debts and shareholder unrest led to liquidation in 1858; assets continued under G. Brandus & S. Dufour. Despite a catalogue exceeding 13,000 works (Meyerbeer, Rossini, Berlioz, Offenbach) the firm never shook its debt; after Dufour and Gemmy Brandus died (1873) Louis resumed control, but bankruptcy followed in 1887.
- **Significance** – Demonstrates how joint‑stock finance could enable rapid catalogue growth yet expose a publisher to over‑leverage and shareholder pressure.
