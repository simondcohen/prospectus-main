## Research Framework

Examining "trafic honteux" (shameful traffic) through complementary mechanisms:

- Criminalization (street music policing)
- Commercialization (café-concert licensing)
- Focus on 1863 as "sorting" moment for legitimate vs illegitimate musical bodies/spaces

### Key Research Questions for Archives:

- How did SACEM navigate rights for café-concert mixed programs?
- Were women café singers subject to police des mœurs registration?
- What behavioral clauses appear in notarial contracts for women performers?
- How did port cities (Bordeaux, Marseille) adapt Parisian regulatory models?
- What financial networks supported Italian child musician trafficking?

**What you'll find:** Membership applications, work-declaration ("bulletin de déclaration") forms and correspondence files stretching back to the 1850s—exactly the material needed to track the Eldorado dispute. The Musée SACEM's online catalogue lets you filter results by decade (choose "1860s").

**Status: Verified**

The most recent *État des fonds* lists sous-série **DA 834 – "Musiciens ambulants"**, covering individual permits, noise-complaint dossiers and the 1863 arrêtés on mechanical music; consult it before requesting cartons.

## Gallica Digitised Resources
